JUMPER’S KNEE PROTOCOL 
The Protocol 
 
Fixing Jumper’s Knee 
In the science section of Jumper’s Knee Protocol, two methods of patellar tendon rehab were 
laid out: 1) a 4-stage process and 2) heavy isometrics.  Which one should you use?  Both. 
 
By combining the principles of each method, you are addressing the tendon but also 1) the 
brain, 2) the muscle, and 3) the kinetic chain. 
 
1) Brain 
The brain puts pain in your patellar tendon to protect it.  This is a good 
thing because pain forces you to stop loading your compromised 
tendon.  If you had no pain and continued to load, you would risk 
rupture.  But it’s also a bad thing if you want to get back to playing sport 
normally. 
 
The first step in restoring normal brain function is stage 1 isometrics.  
This type of loading helps to safely put load back through the tendon while balancing out the 
problems of inhibition and activation coming from the brain. 
 
You can get a synergistic effect if you use auditory (metronome), visual (eye movement), and 
sensory (putting your hands on the quadriceps muscles to feel them contract).  These all help to 
accelerate the process of restoring normal brain function.  
 
2) 
Muscle 
The quadriceps and the calves are two areas that will show loss of size, 
strength, and function with patellar tendinopathy.  The entire limb is also 
important, but the knee extensors show the biggest loss in function.  Stage 
2 lifting becomes about strengthening your 
atrophied muscles. 
 
3) Kinetic Chain 
Pain changes how the body moves.  Jumps become more of a hip hinge, 
lands become stiffer at the knees, and the whole leg can tend to be 
more extended during accelerations, decelerations, and changes of 
direction.  Brain and muscle improvements in stage 1 and 2 will help to 
restore normal kinetic chain function but stage 3 store-and-release is 
when you put everything back together and push your elastic function. 
 
The 4-Stage Process 
Rehab is about climbing the ladder.  As pain and function improve in isometrics, 
move up to lifting.  As pain and function improve in lifting, move up to store-
and-release.  As pain and function improve in store-and-release, move up to 
sport.  The benchmarks for progression are listed at the end of each phase.

Managing Pain 
Patellar tendon rehab is difficult because there is no specific plan or exercise that will work for 
all people.  But patellar tendon rehab is also easy because the tendon tells you if it’s happy or 
not with pain responses.  You just need to know when to listen: 
During training.  Increased tendon pain during training, as long as it’s not excruciating, is okay.  
It’s a normal part of rehab.  An allowable pain number that’s consistent across the research is 
a 3/10 pain.  Keep in mind that this is a rough estimate.  Some people do better on the low 
end (1/10), and some do better on the high end (5/10).  How do you know where you fit?  The 
next day pain response. 
Next Day Pain.  Increased tendon pain 24-hours later or the following morning is not okay.  
This is a sign that the tendon’s capacity has been exceeded. 
Next day pain is always the guiding light.  This will tell you whether your tendon is tolerating 
rehab well or if you need to scale back.  To determine next day pain, you have two options: 
1) Pain-Provocation Test 
This is the single-leg decline squat done to 90° of knee flexion or 
maximum angle allowed by pain. 
You do 1 repetition every single day at the same time and you keep 
note of your pain level. 
If your pain has returned to baseline from the previous day, this means 
the previous day’s loading has been tolerated.  You can progress as 
planned. 
If the pain is worse, load tolerance has been exceeded.  This is when it 
may be necessary to scale back by moving up a stage (e.g., Stage 2 Lifting back to Stage 
1 Isometrics), decreasing the intensity (e.g., jumps at 50% instead of 100%), or 
decreasing the volume (e.g., 5 sets to 3 sets) until pain settles. 
2) Next Morning Pain 
The pain provocation test can be reliable if done at the 
same time everyday under the same conditions.  
Sometimes, this can be difficult to pull off.  For this reason, 
next morning pain can be a more accurate measure of how 
your tendon is doing. 
Getting out of bed and walking around for a bit can be 
enough to gauge how the tendon is doing from the previous day’s loading.  Increased 
stiffness and pain compared to the previous morning is a sign that your tendon’s 
capacity has been exceeded and training needs to be scaled back.

Foot Pressures: 
If you have patellar tendinopathy, you probably “toe grip” the ground.  This is not a good thing.  
When performing isometric exercises, you need to avoid this.  For most movements where the 
foot is on the ground, you should keep your weight on the tripod: Heel, first metatarsal head, 
and fifth metatarsal head.  This will add strength to the foot arches, prevent toe gripping, and 
help to normalize skeletal positions up the kinetic chain.

1) QUADRICEPS ISOMETRIC 
Choose one of the following exercises.  You should feel a big hit on the quadriceps musculature 
and not too much discomfort on the knee itself.  Do 5 sets of 30-45 second holds with a weight 
or depth that puts you at near-failure upon completion of the time: 
- 
Single-leg Leg Extension.  Use a 60-degree knee angle for patellar tendinopathy,          
90-degrees for quadriceps tendinopathy, and 90-degrees for patellar tendinopathy at the tibial 
tuberosity (area of Osgood-Schlatter).  Two options: Hang your leg off a bench and hold weight 
in the foot or get on a leg extension machine. 
- 
Single-leg Leg Press.  Same angles as above.  Lower down to the position and hold. 
- 
Spanish Squat.  Anywhere from 60-90 degrees (or whatever you can tolerate) while 
maintaining an upright torso. 
- 
Single-leg Wall Sit.  Same as Spanish Squat. 
 
 
Other options: Single-leg Sissy Squat (if well-tolerated) and Reverse Nordic (can be 
useful for quadriceps tendinopathy because of deep knee flexion).

2) FOAM ROLLER BRIDGE  
This is a 3-part progression.  Stay on the first and fifth metatarsal heads, not the toes.  The 
glutes should come off the ground a bit but not too high to where you feel it in your low back.  
Initially, you will feel a big hit to the calves and hamstrings.  As tolerance improves, these holds 
should become easy.  Once you can do an exercise with ease for the 30 seconds with 3 sets, 
move on to the next one in the following session: 
- 
Double-leg Bridge.  Two feet on the foam roller.  Do a calf raise so the heels come 
away from the floor.  Hold. 
- 
Single-leg Bridge.  Same position but now take one leg off the foam roller and raise it 
into the air.  Do a calf raise so the heel comes away from the floor.  Hold. 
- 
Single-leg Bridge w/ calf raises.  Same position as the single-leg bridge but now, do 
slow and controlled calf raises (plantarflexion and dorsiflexion) for the length of the set.

3) ISO LUNGE/SPLIT SQUAT    
You have three options.  Choose only one in a workout.  Accumulate 3 min. on each side.  
Attempt to get the time in one set.  If technique starts to falter, stop that set and rest.  If you 
can’t get the 3 min. all at once, take a break, do the other side, and keep going until you 
accumulate the time on each side.  Start from standing and “pull” yourself down into position 
for all of these isometrics: 
- 
ISO Lunge.  Long stride, front knee at around 90-degrees, chest up, weight 
through the tripod of the foot, try to lengthen out the back leg’s hip, hold. 
- 
ISO Split Squat.  Shorter stride where the back knee is just under the hip, front 
knee at around 60- to 90-degrees, chest up, back glute squeezed, weight through the 
tripod of the foot, hold.  This will be a bit more difficult than the ISO Lunge and you 
should feel a big hit on the back leg’s rectus femoris muscle. 
- 
ISO Rear Foot Elevated Split Squat.  Back foot up on a bench behind you, 
front knee at around 60- to 90-degrees, chest up, weight through the tripod of the foot, 
hold.  This will be more difficult than the ISO Split Squat because most of the weight will 
be on the front foot.

Troubleshooting Stage 1 Isometrics: 
 
Stage 1 Isometric training is meant to be done as a standalone.  You need to completely remove 
everything else so you can build yourself back up.  There are some exceptions listed below. 
 
If there is something you can do that barely loads the patellar tendon at all (e.g., getting 
standing shots up for a basketball player, light straight-line dribbling for a soccer player, easy 
skipping for a track and field jumper), then go ahead.  If you get increased pain responses from 
any activity added, you need to remove it.  You are free to do whatever upper body training you 
want. 
 
If you think you have a very bad tendon and it hasn’t been loaded this extreme in a while, you 
should go a bit lighter on the quadriceps isometrics to start off and increase difficulty over time. 
 
If you can’t do the full workout twice per day, here are some options: 
- 
One full session and one session with just the quadriceps isometric. 
- 
One full session and one session with just exercise 3 & 4 (bodyweight 
isometrics). 
- 
One session with just the quadricep isometric and everything else done in a 
different session. 
 
Warmup for these sessions should look like this: 
- 
30-45 second Quadriceps isometric hold with a light weight (e.g., Leg Extension) 
or easier position (e.g., Spanish Squat). 
- 
30-45 second Quadriceps isometric hold with a medium weight (e.g., Leg 
Extension) or slightly more difficult position (e.g., Spanish Squat). 
- 
Begin the session.  Those two warmup sets do not count towards the 5 sets. 
 
You can mix up the exercises between sessions or you can keep them the same.  It’s personal 
preference.  There are pros and cons to either choice.  See what works best for you. 
 
If you are getting a lot of Vastus Lateralis (outside quad muscle) tightness, you 
should work to decrease the tone of that area.  Do these two exercises either 
before or after the isometric session: 
- 
Foam Roll Vastus Lateralis.  Go up and down the outside quad in a 
controlled fashion, stopping in spots where there is tightness.  2 minutes 
per side. 
- 
Couch stretch.  Back knee up against the wall, push the hip forward 
so it stretches the quads.  Start with the hands on the 
floor.  If possible, sit straight up.  Hold 2 minutes per side.

Twist in the Knee.  If you feel that the leg gets locked easily, you could have a “twist in the 
knee”.  The isometric sessions themselves will help to clear this up but you can use a more 
focused approach to restore what’s missing.  Run through this sequence of simple fixes before 
an isometric session to unlock the knee by working upstream and downstream: 
1) Calcaneal Eversion.  20 reps each side.  Grab the heel bone and forcefully push it outwards, 
away from midline. 
2) Tibial Internal Rotation.  20 reps each side.  Get in a lunge position with your hands around 
the upper calf and tibial tuberosity.  As you push your hip forward, try to internally rotate 
the tibia.  As you come back, let go of the pull.  Repeat for reps. 
3) Wall Single-leg Bridge.  30 seconds each side.  Keep the foot’s tripod on the wall, elevating 
the heel on a foam roller.  Come up slightly with the hips and keep a little bend in the knee.  
Bring the opposite knee up, bring your opposite elbow to that knee, hold, and take deep 
breaths. 
4) 90/90 Hip Lift.  1 minute.  Heels on a bench, something between the knees to engage the 
adductors, posteriorly tilt the pelvis using your upper hamstrings.  Hold the position and 
breathe. 
5) 90/90 hip switches.  20 reps each side.  Try to stay as upright as possible.  When range of 
motion allows, try not using the hands for support. 
6) Arm bar.  20 reps each side.  Grab a 25-50 pound weight.  Arm straight up, rotate to your 
side by bringing your knee up.  Keep the opposite leg straight.  Rock back and repeat. 
7) Bench Adductor.  20 reps each side.  Bodyweight.  Knee or foot on a bench.  Side plank 
position.  Bring the hips up off the ground by engaging the adductors (groin), then let the 
hips go back down. 
8) Full Squat.  2 minutes.  Grab on to something for balance.  Squat down as deep as you can.  
Keep the heels down but let the knees travel forward.  Try inhaling and exhaling into the 
back of your ribcage. 
9) DB Side Bend.  20 reps each side.  Grab a weight that’s heavy but not so heavy that you 
can’t move your spine fully.  Bend to the side and stand up.  Repeat for reps. 
10) Hanging Knee Raise.  20 reps.  Hanging from a bar.  Bring the knees up so your thighs come 
above 90 degrees and your pelvis posteriorly rotates.  Straighten out and repeat. 
 
Done together, these 10 movements will help to restore normal range of motion in your lower 
body while integrating musculature so you can “untwist the knee”.  After running through this 
sequence, you should feel much better in the patellar tendon even though you didn’t load it.

WHEN TO PROGRESS 
 
BENCHMARKS TO ENTER STAGE 2 LIFTING: 
 
• Next day pain is regularly at a 3/10 or less, using either: 
➢ How you feel in the morning 
➢ Pain-provocation test 
• Improvements in Quadriceps, Calves, and Entire Leg function: 
➢ Your QUADRICEPS ISOMETRIC numbers have noticeably improved 
➢ You can manage doing the FOAM ROLLER BRIDGE on a single leg with calf raises 
➢ Your ISO LUNGE/SPLIT SQUAT and SINGLE-LEG STAND times are noticeably improved 
(e.g., you are at or near being able to hold for 3-minutes straight). 
• Strength and endurance of the legs are similar side-to-side. 
You will still use this same isometric workout for the subsequent stages.  For the quadriceps 
isometric, continue to get stronger.  For the foam roller bridge (once you hit the last 
progression), alternate each progression between workouts (e.g., isometric day 1 – 3 sets 
double leg, isometric day 2 – 3 sets single-leg, isometric day 3, 3 sets single-leg w/ calf raises, 
repeat sequence).  For the iso lunge/split squat and single-leg stand, you should get to the 
point where 3-minutes is the norm and you’ll just keep doing that for each workout.

1) ACHILLES HOPPING PROGRESSION  
This is a 7-part progression.  Do only one exercise per session.  In your very first workout, start 
with the first exercise.  You can progress to the next exercise in the following session if you can 
do the exercise for the sets and reps with good ankle stiffness (e.g., your heel doesn’t drop, you

least 1 minute between sets or until fully recovered: 
1) Band-Assisted Double-leg Pogos. Do 4 sets of 25 reps. 
2) Double-leg Pogos. Do 4 sets of 20 reps. 
3) Alternate-leg Pogos. Do 4 sets of 10 reps each leg. 
4) Single-leg Pogos. Do 4 sets of 10 reps each leg. 
5) Low Hurdle Hopping. Do 4 sets over 10 low hurdles. 
6) Medium Hurdle Hopping. Do 4 sets over 10 medium hurdles. 
7) Double-leg tuck jumps. Do 4 sets of 5 reps. 
8) Single-leg tuck jumps. Do 4 sets of 3 reps per leg.

2) KNEE-DOMINANT SQUAT  
 
Pick a squat variation that you tolerate well and can progressively 
add load to over time.  If you find that you really compensate to load 
the hips more than the knees, you can elevate the heels.  Think of 
dropping your pelvis straight down and bringing it straight up.  This 
will load the knees, which is what you want out of a squat.  Control 
down 3 seconds and come up for 3 seconds.  Do a couple warmup 
sets until the weight is heavy enough.  You should have only 2-3 reps 
left in the tank when the set is done.  Week 1 is 5 reps.  Week 2 is 4 
reps.  Week 3 is 3 reps. 3 minutes rest between sets. 
- 
Goblet Squat. 
- 
Front Squat. 
- 
Back Squat. 
- 
Zercher Squat.

3) SPLIT SQUAT  
 
Pick a split squat variation that you tolerate well and can progressively add load to over time.  
The front knee should always bend to around 90 degrees.  The knee can remain over the ankle 
when the patellar tendon is really sore, but if it’s not, you can push the knee a bit over the toes.  
As with the squat, think of dropping your hips straight down and bringing them straight up.  
Control down 3 seconds and come up for 3 seconds.  Do a couple warmup sets until the weight 
is heavy enough.  You should have only 2-3 reps left in the tank when the set is done.  Week 1 is 
5 reps each side.  Week 2 is 4 reps each side.  Week 3 is 3 reps each side. 3 minutes rest 
between sets. 
- 
Front Foot Elevated Split Squat.

Split Squat. 
- 
Rear Foot Elevated Split Squat.

4) LATERAL LUNGE  
 
Hold only one weight in the inside hand.  Step to the side, sit back, bend the knee, keep the 
chest up.  Try to keep the sternum over the knee and the knee over the ankle.  Push back 
through the foot, coming back to center and repeating again.  This is not an exercise to load 
heavy, it’s to get internal rotation of the hip and pronation of the foot.  3 sets of 10 reps each.  
Don’t worry about progressively overloading, just try to keep the movement smooth with the 
sternum-knee-foot relationship.  If you take too big of a step, it will be difficult to keep those 
areas lined up.  In that case, take a small step until your range of motion opens up.  You should 
feel the adductors (groin) working with each rep.  Rest as needed between sets.

JUMPER’S KNEE PROTOCOL 
Troubleshooting Stage 2 Lifting: 
 
In Stage 1, zero to minimal patellar tendon stress activities were allowed.  Now that you’re in 
Stage 2, you can push the intensity a bit more to keep working on sport skills: 
- 
Standing basketball shots can now become shots on the move. 
- 
Light straight line soccer dribbling can now become dribbling around cones. 
- 
Easy skipping for a jumper can now become a bit more intense. 
 
If pain increases from these, regress.  You can do whatever upper body training you want. 
 
For the strength exercises, it is a 3-week sequence of sets/reps.  If you complete the 3-weeks 
and you still need more time in Stage 2 Lifting because pain is still high and/or you’re still weak, 
start over at week one.  Keep repeating this until you hit the criteria for progression listed at 
the bottom of this page. 
 
Warmup for Isometric days follow the same recommendation as listed in Stage 1 Isometrics.  
Since Lifting days start with the Achilles Hopping Progression, you should warmup a bit more to 
prepare for that.  This is also a good chance to get more low-level store-and-release work out of 
your lower body.  Do this: 
- 
Jog 100 yards 
- 
Backwards jog 100 yards 
- 
Shuffle 100 yards each 
- 
Carioca 100 yards each 
- 
Skip 100 yards  
 
Or, if you have training partners, play spikeball until warm.  This will get unconscious, low-level 
spring activity from the Patellar and Achilles tendons. 
 
Outside of the Achilles Hopping Progression (which needs to be done in order as pain and 
function allow), you can alternate strength exercises between sessions if you prefer.  Squats, 
split squats, hip hinges, step ups can all be interchanged or kept the exact same.  There are pros 
and cons to either choice.  See what works for you, just make sure you’re consistently getting 
stronger over the weeks. 
Tempo: 
 
The suggested tempo to strengthen muscle and to continue tendon stress relaxation is the 3-
seconds eccentric and 3-seconds concentric.  You should always begin your lifting phase with 
this slow tempo.  If you are tolerating this well and gaining strength, you have two options:

DAY 1 (STORE-AND-RELEASE): 
 
What follows is a general recommendation for going about the Store-And-Release Day.  Stage 1 
Isometrics and Stage 2 Lifting are similar across the board for all athletes.  Stage 3 Store-And-
Release is much more individual. 
 
The patterns affected by patellar tendinopathy are landing, jumping, acceleration/deceleration, 
and change of direction.  Some athletes need more of some and less of others.  For example: 
 
- 
A high jumper’s program will include a lot of jumping and acceleration 
- 
A soccer player’s program will include a lot of acceleration, deceleration, and change of direction 
- 
A basketball player’s program will include a lot of everything 
- 
An American football punter’s program will include kicking variations 
- 
A tennis player’s program will include a lot of change of direction and deceleration 
- 
A Powerlifter will not need to do this phase because they only lift 
 
This Store-And-Release program will address mostly everything an athlete will need.  But if you 
feel you need more specific preparation to get back to sport, tweak the exercises and 
progressions to meet your demands. 
 
What follows are 12 workouts.  If you complete workout #1 with what you feel is good 
technique (Function) and it doesn’t hurt more the following day (Pain), then you can progress 
to workout #2 in the following Store-And-Release session.  If you do not perform the workout 
with good function and/or you get increased pain the following day, stay at that given workout 
for another session until you can do it with good function and no pain increase. 
 
The progressions are explained first, and the twelve individual workouts are explained later.

Troubleshooting Stage 3 Store-And-Release: 
 
Minimal stress sport skill activities were allowed in Stage 2.  Now that you’re in Stage 3, you can 
make these changes to continue progressing with sport: 
- 
Basketball shots on the move can become shots against a defender. 
- 
Soccer dribbling around cones can now become dribbling against a defender. 
- 
Skipping for a jumper can become light bounding. 
 
The level of effort will depend on what you’re ready to handle.  If pain increases from these 
changes, pull back the intensity or go back to the well-tolerated movement you did in Stage 2.  
You have free reign on upper body training. 
 
Warmup the same as you would do for the Achilles Hopping progression.  Running variations 
for distance, a low-level game like spikeball, etc.  Just make sure you’re completely warm and 
the lower body feels ready for store-and-release movements. 
 
For every exercise, do 2-3 warmup sets where you go through the motion of the exercise at a 
lower intensity. 
 
Rest as long as needed between sets.  In the first 6 workouts or so, you probably won’t need as 
much rest because the intensity is 50-70%.  1-2 minutes should be enough.  In the last 6 
workouts or so, you will probably need more rest because the intensity is 90-100%.  2-3 
minutes will probably be right. 
 
The weekly setup goes 1) Store-And-Release, 2) Isometrics, and 3) Lifting.  This is because day 1 
is most likely to provoke pain and discomfort the following day, so isometrics can settle this.  By 
day 3, you should be feeling pretty good to do lifting and then ready for day 1 again.  If you are 
not ready, you can take a rest day or do another isometric day while you wait to feel ready.  If 
you have setbacks, don’t panic.  They’re a normal part of Stage 3. 
 
In the latter half of your Stage 3, you should really be using the sporting implement as much as 
possible.  Add it into your jumping, landing, accelerating, decelerating, and changing direction 
movements to further prepare you for Stage 4 Return to Sport.

10 Things to Keep in Mind 
 
1) The Brain deficit.  You need to spend enough time with isometrics (and visual, 
auditory, sensory tools) to normalize the brain’s inhibition and activation. 
 
2) The Muscle deficit.  You need to spend enough time strengthening your muscles 
with lifting (e.g., squats, split squats, hip hinges, step ups, calf raises). 
 
3) The Soleus-Hamstring deficit.  You need to get good at developing co-contractions 
around the knee (e.g., foam roller bridge, hopping progression). 
 
4) The Hopping deficit.  You need to develop the ankle/foot/Achilles.  If that area has a 
deficit, the knee will be forced to deal with it and you’ll likely have setbacks. 
 
5) Eat right.  You get tendon and muscle adaptations when you recover from training.  
Eat enough protein (~1 gram per pound bodyweight per day) from a variety of 
sources.  Eat enough carbohydrates and fats to provide you energy.  Specifically for 
tendons, consume some proteins higher in proline and glycine (e.g., collagen) and 
make sure you get enough vitamin C (e.g., oranges). 
 
6) Sleep enough.  There is some evidence in vitro and in animal models that tendon 
accumulates damage throughout the day and cleans itself up at night.  When the 
circadian rhythm is interrupted, this can cause issues with tendon clean up and 
could potentially be the start of tendinopathy.  Try to stay consistent with sleep-and-
wake times and get ~8 hours per night. 
 
7) Move enough.  A Jumper’s Knee Protocol training sessions lasts ~1 hour.  If you lay 
on the couch the other 23 hours, do you think that’s going to help you?  Probably 
not.  Motion is lotion and chronically high blood sugar (potentially from a sedentary 
lifestyle) can negatively impact tendon health.  Get 10,000 steps per day. 
 
8) Don’t change loads fast.  If you go from 0 jumps in one week to 100 jumps the next 
week, you will probably blow up your tendon.  Be slow with how you change store-
and-release loads. 
 
9) Appreciate the tendon healing process.  The tendinopathy continuum says that a 
normal tendon goes reactive, then into tendon dysrepair, then into a degenerative 
tendon.  Along the path, it will tell you how it’s feeling if you listen.  Some tendons 
take longer to heal than others.  If you had an event where you really aggravated the 
tendon, the reactive response could last for weeks or months.  Tendons don’t have a 
set recovery time.  Listen to next day pain and give the tendon what it wants.   
 
10) Be aware of psychosocial factors.  Look into the biopsychosocial model of pain.  
Thoughts, emotions, and behaviors can positively or negatively impact rehab.  
Economical, environmental, and cultural factors can do the same.
